# ISO 27001 ↔ NIS2 Mapping

## Objective

This document demonstrates how ISO/IEC 27001 can support NIS2-related security governance requirements within modern organizations.

The goal is not a one-to-one legal interpretation, but a governance-oriented alignment approach.

It shows how an existing ISMS can help organizations structure cyber risk management, incident response, business continuity, supplier security, and management accountability in a way that supports regulatory readiness.

---

## Why This Matters

Organizations rarely implement a single framework in isolation.

In reality, companies often need to align:

- ISO/IEC 27001
- NIS2
- GDPR
- DORA
- BSI IT-Grundschutz
- internal governance requirements
- customer and audit requirements

Without harmonization, this can lead to duplicated controls, disconnected documentation, unclear responsibilities, and inefficient audits.

A structured mapping approach helps organizations reduce duplication, improve audit readiness, and build a consistent governance model across multiple security and compliance requirements.

---

## Governance Perspective

ISO/IEC 27001 provides the management system foundation for information security.

NIS2 introduces regulatory obligations focused on cyber risk management, resilience, incident reporting, supply chain security, and management accountability.

From a governance perspective, ISO 27001 can act as the operational backbone that helps organizations implement, document, monitor, and improve many NIS2-related requirements.

The key idea is simple:

> ISO 27001 provides structure.  
> NIS2 increases regulatory expectations.  
> Governance mapping connects both into a practical operating model.

## Key Alignment Areas

### 1. Risk Management

NIS2 places strong emphasis on appropriate cyber risk management measures.

ISO 27001 supports this through a structured ISMS risk management process, including:

- risk identification
- risk analysis
- risk evaluation
- risk treatment
- residual risk handling
- management acceptance

This makes ISO 27001 a strong foundation for demonstrating a risk-based approach to information security governance.

---

### 2. Incident Management

NIS2 increases expectations regarding incident handling, escalation, and reporting.

ISO 27001 supports this through defined incident management processes, including:

- incident detection
- reporting channels
- escalation paths
- roles and responsibilities
- response coordination
- lessons learned
- continual improvement

A mature incident response process helps organizations move from ad hoc reaction to structured decision-making during security events.

---

### 3. Supply Chain Security

NIS2 explicitly strengthens the importance of supply chain and third-party security.

ISO 27001 supports supplier governance through controls and processes related to:

- supplier selection
- contractual security requirements
- access restrictions
- service monitoring
- third-party risk assessments
- supplier review cycles

This is especially relevant for organizations relying on IT service providers, cloud platforms, managed service providers, or outsourced business processes.

---

### 4. Business Continuity and Resilience

NIS2 focuses strongly on operational resilience.

ISO 27001 supports this through business continuity and recovery-oriented controls, including:

- backup concepts
- recovery planning
- crisis communication
- continuity procedures
- disaster recovery considerations
- regular testing and improvement

The governance goal is not only to prevent incidents, but to ensure the organization remains capable of operating during and after disruptions.

---

### 5. Management Accountability

NIS2 increases pressure on management bodies to understand, approve, and oversee cyber risk management.

ISO 27001 supports this through:

- leadership commitment
- defined responsibilities
- management reviews
- risk acceptance decisions
- resource allocation
- performance monitoring
- continual improvement

This creates a direct connection between information security governance and executive accountability.

---

## Executive Interpretation

Framework mapping is not just a compliance activity.

It is a governance exercise.

A mature organization should not manage ISO 27001, NIS2, DORA, BSI, and internal policies as isolated compliance silos.

Instead, it should build an integrated governance model where one security control can support multiple requirements.

This improves:

- audit efficiency
- management transparency
- regulatory readiness
- control consistency
- operational resilience
- risk-based decision-making

The practical value of ISO 27001 in a NIS2 context is that it provides a structured management system that can be used to organize, document, and continuously improve cyber security governance.

---

## Example Governance Scenario

A company operates an ISO 27001-aligned ISMS and becomes subject to NIS2-related requirements.

Instead of creating a separate NIS2 compliance program from scratch, the organization can use its existing ISMS as the foundation.

Example approach:

1. Review the existing ISMS scope.
2. Identify NIS2-relevant business processes and systems.
3. Map existing ISO 27001 controls to NIS2 requirement areas.
4. Identify gaps in incident reporting, supplier security, resilience, and management accountability.
5. Update the risk register and treatment plan.
6. Adjust policies, procedures, and reporting structures.
7. Prepare management reporting and audit evidence.
8. Monitor effectiveness through internal reviews and continual improvement.

This approach avoids duplicate governance structures and strengthens the existing security management system.

## Summary

ISO 27001 and NIS2 are not identical, but they complement each other well.

ISO 27001 provides the structured management system.

NIS2 increases regulatory expectations around cyber risk, resilience, supply chain security, incident reporting, and management accountability.

By mapping both perspectives, organizations can create an integrated governance model that supports compliance, audit readiness, and better security decision-making.

The purpose of this mapping is to demonstrate how regulatory requirements can be connected to practical ISMS artifacts and real-world security governance.
