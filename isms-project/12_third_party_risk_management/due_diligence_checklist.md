# Supplier Due Diligence Checklist

## Objective

This document demonstrates a governance-oriented supplier due diligence approach used to evaluate third-party security, governance maturity, operational resilience, and compliance readiness before onboarding or reviewing external suppliers.

The objective is to support:

- risk-informed supplier onboarding
- operational resilience
- audit readiness
- governance accountability
- regulatory alignment
- proportional supplier oversight

Due diligence activities help organizations identify potential supplier-related risks before formal business integration or contractual engagement.

---

## Why Due Diligence Matters

Third-party suppliers may introduce risks related to:

- sensitive data exposure
- operational disruption
- weak security governance
- insufficient resilience capabilities
- regulatory non-compliance
- privileged system access
- unmanaged subcontractor dependencies

Without structured due diligence processes, organizations may onboard suppliers without understanding their actual governance and security maturity.

This can increase:

- operational risk exposure
- compliance risks
- resilience weaknesses
- incident response challenges
- audit findings

A structured due diligence process helps organizations improve supplier transparency and governance consistency.

---

## Governance Perspective

Supplier due diligence is not only a procurement activity.

It is part of enterprise governance and risk management.

The purpose is to evaluate whether suppliers meet minimum expectations regarding:

- security governance
- operational resilience
- compliance maturity
- access management
- incident handling
- risk management
- contractual accountability

The depth of due diligence activities should be proportionate to supplier classification and operational criticality.

## Example Governance Workflow

1. Identify supplier relationship and service scope.
2. Determine supplier classification and criticality.
3. Define proportional due diligence requirements.
4. Review supplier documentation and governance evidence.
5. Assess identified risks and governance gaps.
6. Define mitigation requirements if necessary.
7. Document approval decisions and ownership.
8. Schedule periodic supplier review activities.

This process supports consistent and risk-informed supplier onboarding and governance oversight.

---

## Example Governance Outcomes

| Assessment Result | Example Governance Action |
|---|---|
| Acceptable Risk | Proceed with onboarding |
| Minor Governance Gaps | Define remediation requirements |
| Elevated Operational Risk | Increase monitoring and oversight |
| Critical Governance Weaknesses | Escalate for management review |
| Insufficient Transparency | Delay onboarding until evidence is provided |

---

## Proportional Governance Principle

Not all suppliers require identical governance activities.

Due diligence depth should scale according to:

- operational dependency
- data sensitivity
- regulatory relevance
- privileged access exposure
- resilience impact
- business criticality

This helps organizations allocate governance resources efficiently while maintaining risk-informed oversight.

## Executive Interpretation

Mature organizations should not rely solely on contractual trust when onboarding suppliers.

Instead, governance-oriented due diligence processes should validate whether suppliers meet appropriate security, resilience, and operational expectations before critical dependencies are established.

This improves transparency, resilience, and long-term governance maturity across supplier ecosystems.

## Summary

Supplier due diligence is a foundational component of Third-Party Risk Management.

It helps organizations evaluate supplier governance maturity, identify operational and security risks, and support risk-informed onboarding decisions.
