# Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM)

## Overview

Modern organizations increasingly rely on external suppliers, cloud providers, SaaS platforms, managed service providers, and outsourced operational services.

As dependencies on third parties grow, organizations must ensure that external relationships do not introduce uncontrolled operational, security, compliance, or resilience risks.

This section demonstrates how Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) can be integrated into a structured governance and ISMS approach.

The focus is on governance-oriented supplier oversight aligned with:

- ISO/IEC 27001
- NIS2
- DORA
- operational resilience requirements
- audit readiness
- enterprise risk management

---

## Why Third-Party Risk Management Matters

Third parties often have direct or indirect access to:

- sensitive information
- critical business systems
- operational processes
- customer data
- infrastructure services
- privileged access environments

Weak supplier governance can lead to:

- supply chain attacks
- operational disruption
- compliance violations
- data breaches
- resilience failures
- audit findings
- reputational damage

Modern governance frameworks increasingly require organizations to assess, monitor, and manage supplier-related risks in a structured and documented manner.

---

## Governance Perspective

Third-Party Risk Management is not only a procurement activity.

It is a governance capability.

The objective is to ensure that supplier relationships are:

- risk-assessed
- classified
- monitored
- contractually governed
- periodically reviewed
- aligned with organizational security expectations

A mature TPRM program supports both operational resilience and regulatory readiness.

---

## Governance Objectives

The main objectives of Third-Party Risk Management include:

- reducing supplier-related security risks
- improving visibility into external dependencies
- strengthening operational resilience
- supporting audit readiness
- improving governance accountability
- aligning supplier controls with business risk
- supporting regulatory compliance obligations

---

## Core Governance Areas

| Governance Area | Objective |
|---|---|
| Vendor Classification | Prioritize suppliers based on risk and criticality |
| Risk Assessment | Identify and evaluate supplier-related risks |
| Due Diligence | Validate supplier security and governance maturity |
| Contractual Controls | Define security and compliance expectations |
| Access Governance | Restrict and monitor supplier access |
| Monitoring & Review | Continuously evaluate supplier risk exposure |
| Incident Coordination | Define escalation and communication procedures |
| Operational Resilience | Ensure continuity during supplier disruptions |
| Audit Support | Maintain evidence and governance documentation |

## Example Governance Scenario

An organization relies on multiple external service providers including:

- cloud infrastructure providers
- SaaS platforms
- outsourced IT support
- managed security services
- external software vendors

Without structured governance, supplier-related risks may remain unmanaged or undocumented.

A governance-oriented TPRM approach may include:

1. Classifying suppliers by criticality and business impact.
2. Performing supplier risk assessments.
3. Reviewing security documentation and certifications.
4. Defining contractual security requirements.
5. Restricting and monitoring supplier access.
6. Conducting periodic governance reviews.
7. Defining incident escalation procedures.
8. Maintaining audit evidence and risk documentation.

This helps organizations reduce operational risk while improving resilience and compliance readiness.
